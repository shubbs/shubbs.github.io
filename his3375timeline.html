HIS3375 Timeline: The Role of Race in Canadian Public Education

Introduction

This timeline shows the role that race has played in the context of public education in Canada. Given that public education in Canada is provincially legislated, the focus of this timeline is limited.

The focus lies primarily on people, events, and legislation in British Columbia, Nova Scotia, and Ontario, specifically relating to the experiences of Chinese Canadians, Japanese Canadians, and African-Canadian/Black Canadians. It is important to note that experiences across Canada for different people of colour have differed greatly, and this timeline does not speak to all of these experiences.

The intent of this timeline is to draw attention to the reality of school segregation in Canada and the acts of resistance by children, youth, and families to segregation. Further, it hopes to build understanding around contemporary and contentious issues such as the creation of Africentric schools in Toronto. By shedding light on the history of segregation in Canada, we can stop equating Africentric schools to enforced segregation.

A significant gap in this timeline that is essential to include in the discussion of race and education in Canada is the history of the Indian Residential School System. Timelines of the Residential School System can be found through the NCTR here and here, and a condensed timeline was created by the Aboriginal Healing Foundation here. These sources must be brought to attention in order to gain a more complete understanding of the role that race has played in the history of educating children and youth in Canada. To avoid glossing over the complexities of this system and the experiences of survivors, this timeline does not cover the Residential School System.

  • < 1800s Before Mass Schooling

    Before the 19th century, education is seen as a privilege of the aristocracy.[1] Ideas of mass schooling begin to take root later in the century, with goals of addressing social issues, increasing literacy rates, and “mitigating influence of religious authorities.”[2]

  • Mid–1800s Public Education

    The establishment of a public education system that perpetuates “fundamental British-Canadian myths of moral superiority and universality.”[3] People begin paying taxes for education, which will become an issue as people of colour are refused entry to common schools.

  • 1840 Segregated Schols in Chatham, ON

    Black students are forced to attend segregated schools in Chatham as a result of racist movements by politicians and the public that present African Canadians as “morally inferior.”[4] This is one case of many in which segregated schools are enforced despite desires from African Canadian families to have their children admitted to public schools.

  • 1843 Schools Act Prohibits Segregation

    The Schools Act for Upper Canada in 1843 prohibits segregation.[5] Despite this, efforts are already in place to segregate students. In Hamilton, children are barred from public schools despite their parents having paid taxes. They are able to attend school after petitioning Governor General Lord Elgin, but segregation continues in nearby areas.[6]

  • 1847–1876 Egerton Ryerson

    During this period, Egerton Ryerson is the superintendent of schools in Canada West. He advocates for schools based on Christian and moral principles.[7] Ryerson does not support segregation; however, he also does “not effectively intervene in any cases of exclusion.”[8]

  • 1850 Schools Act Allows Segregation

    The 1850 Schools Act for Upper Canada makes it the duty of school boards and cities to “authorize the establishment of one, or more, Separate schools for Protestants, Roman Catholics, or Coloured people."[9]. In Ontario and Nova Scotia, legislation will usually be used in rural areas. Urban areas often segregate through streaming or city school boundaries.[10]

  • 1865 Nova Scotia Legislation

    Nova Scotia creates legislation similar to that of Upper Canada, permitting segregated schools. Segregated schools will also exist in New Brunswick, Alberta, and Saskatchewan, but without direct legislation.[11]

  • 1867 British North American Act

    Education becomes a provincial responsibility with constitutional guarantees outlined in the BNA Act. Provincial control leads to different provinces continuing to create different forms of legislation relating to segregation.[12]

    Photo: LAC

  • 1871 Compulsory Education in Ontario

    The Common Schools Act of 1871, created while Ryerson is superintendent, makes public schooling free and compulsory for children, ages 7 to 12. Other provinces will follow in making education compulsory.[13]

  • 1891 Resistance in Chatham, ON

    Segregation in Chatham ends as a result of organized resistance by parents. Children are taken to the school of their parents’ choice rather than to town’s designated segregated school.[14]

  • 1900-1920s BC Partial Segregation

    In British Columbia, partial segregation exists in Victoria and Vancouver for younger children of Chinese descent and for Chinese immigrants. There is pressure from the general public to segregate students of higher grades.[15]

  • 1900 Curriculum Approaches to Race

    British Columbian textbooks portray race as scientifically different, hiding notions social constructs. Race performs “the political function of linking Canadian society with the British Empire."[16] Textbooks of this period emphasize “the intrinsic alienness of those racialized as Chinese.[17]

  • 1908 Chinese Canadian Student Expelled

    A Chinese Canadian boy, aged 16, is expelled from Rock Bay School in Victoria “for drawing obscene pictures of white children.”[18] It is not known for certain whether or not the boy commited the act, but the situation serves to further already existing racist sentiments.

  • July 1922 Segregation in Victoria

    The Victoria School Board decides to expand Chinese segregation, banning older students from school and moving younger students to three segregated schools near Chinatown: Rock Bay, King’s Road, and Railway Street.[^19] Chinese parents and Chinese organizations object the board’s decision.

    Photo: Chinese Schoolchildren

  • September 1922 – March 1923 Students’ Strike in Victoria

    A resistance campaign is run against the Victoria School Board’s decision to segregate schools.[19] On the first day of class, racialized Chineses students are “marched” to segregated schools.[20] A designated boy calls out a Chinese phrase and the students disperse, beginning a year-long strike.[21] Segregated classes for racialized Chinese students will continue until the 1940s.[^24]

  • 1942 Japanese Internment

    In conjunction with Japanese Internment in Canada, schools are segregated. Prior to this point, Japanese Canadians had access to public schools.[22]

    One former school teacher from Caslo, BC recounts being asked to take down the Union Jack in her schoolroom. She refuses, stating, “That is my flag. I am a Canadian and I won’t take it down.”[23] (9:20 of above video)

  • 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights

    The UDHR declares education as a human right, necessitating that it be free, promote tolerance among all racial groups, and give parents the right to choose the kind of education for their children.[24] Canada continues to have segregated schools.

  • 1964 Last Segregated School in Ontario Closes

    Ontario repeals the statute authorizing of segregated education.[25] SS 11 Colchester South, the last segregated school in Ontario and in poor conditions, is closed after parent and community petitions.[26]

  • 1970s – 1980s Rise of Black Advocacy Groups

    Throughout these decades, Black advocacy groups in Toronto, such as the African Canadian Heritage Assocation (ACHA) and the Black Education Project, set up “community-based educational programs designed to address the shortcomings of the provincial curriculum.”[27]

    “Parents in the community were angered at the fact that their children were coming home from school crying their eyes out saying that they didn’t want to be Black.” 6 (ibid) –Louis March, communications director of the ACHA (PUT IN IMG)

  • 1978 Toronto Race Relations Act

    With pressure from advocacy groups, the Toronto District School Board develops a Race Relations Policy. It is “the first and most comprehensive race equity policy developed by a North American school board.”[28]

  • 1983 Last Segregated School in Canada Closes

    The last racially segregated school in Canada closes in Nova Scotia: The Mary E. Cornish Memorial School in Lincolnville.[29]

  • 1985 Committee on the Education of Black Children

    The Committee is established by the TSB after a meeting with the Organization of Parents of Black Children. The meeting addressed “the high dropout rate of Black students, the lack of Black teachers, culturally biased IQ tests, low expectations for Black students, and teachers’ lack of knowledge about Black culture and the history of Blacks in Canada.”[30]

  • 1992 Stephen Lewis Report on Race Relations in Ontario

    In June, the report brings public attention to race relations. Commissioned by Ontario Premier Bob Rae, the report states, “It is Black students who are being inappropriately streamed in schools, it is Black kids who are disproportionately dropping out.”[31]

  • 1992 Proposal for Africentric Schools in Toronto

    In November, the African Canadian Community Working Group proposes Black-focused schools to address the identified needs of African Canadian students. The press compares the initiative to historical segregation.[32] The school welcomes students from all backgrounds. Black parents have been calling for Black-focused schools since the 1980s.[33]

  • 2008 Toronto Africentric School

    The Toronto District School Board approves the opening of an Africentric Alternative School and faces backlash from the press. For a concise note on the opening of this school and the problems of press coverage, see Sylvia Hamilton’s “Stories from The Little Black School House,” pages 105–106.

  • 2008–2017 Continutations

    Students and reports point to continued systemic racism against young people of colour in schools.[34] The TDSB reports that over the last 5 years, 48% of expelled students self-identified as black, versus 10% who were white.
